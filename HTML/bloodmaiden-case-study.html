THE CHALLENGE

Blood Maiden is a dark fantasy action game that requires a delicate balance between atmospheric storytelling and functional gameplay interface.

The main challenge was to create an interface that enhances the dark, gothic atmosphere while ensuring players can quickly access critical information during intense combat sequences.

Players need to feel immersed in the dark fantasy world while having immediate access to health, abilities, and inventory without breaking the game's atmospheric experience.

PROBLEM OR GOAL

What were you trying to create? Why?

I wanted to create a narrative website experience that felt like stepping into the world of a premium dark fantasy game — blending visual storytelling, timeline navigation, and character depth while maintaining emotional pacing and immersive tone.

The goal was to showcase Henrietta's transformation from innocent princess to undead anti-hero, while introducing the political horror and magic of the Blood Maiden universe.

CREATIVE STRATEGY & UX DECISIONS

Break down why you structured it the way you did

Narrative Design

I structured the story as a cinematic timeline, beginning with royal peace, rising to betrayal, and climaxing with rebirth. Every section is a chapter in her fall and rise. Emotional pacing was crucial: from warm nostalgia → tragic horror → righteous vengeance.

User Flow

I guided users from intro > timeline > character deep dive > emotional lore > resurrection, using scroll-triggered sections and visual transitions. Each scroll introduces a new layer of the story and tone shift.

UI Design

Color palette: Black, blood red, gold.
Fonts: Gothic serif (Cinzel, Garamond) for a regal yet haunting look.
Imagery: Handpicked and AI-generated gothic visuals to support every chapter.

REFLECTION / WHAT I LEARNED

This project helped me connect the dots between everything I've learned so far about UX and UI design — and gave me a much deeper understanding of how all the pieces fit together in a real, end-to-end experience.

Working on Blood Maiden challenged me to think beyond just visuals. I had to consider structure, pacing, interaction, accessibility, and emotion — all within a consistent narrative system. It showed me what UX really is: not just clean interfaces, but creating meaning, clarity, and connection for the user.

It also pushed me out of my comfort zone in Figma. In earlier projects, I hadn't been the one driving the design tool hands-on, but here I had full ownership. That taught me not just how to use the tool, but how to think systematically — building reusable components, aligning hierarchy, and iterating on structure as the story evolved.

Throughout the process, I realized just how much every design can be improved. And how important it is to remain open, reflective, and adaptable. I became more aware of both what I know and what I still need to grow into.

I also recognize that this project could have gone much further — with more time, I would have expanded interactivity, refined layout flows, and tested more feedback loops. But even within scope, it showed me the potential of combining storytelling and UX in powerful ways.

I would love to take what I've learned and apply it to a real-world project — for a game studio, a narrative website, or any product that blends interaction with emotional depth. Because this is the kind of work I want to keep doing: designing experiences that are not only functional but unforgettable.
